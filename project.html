PORTFOLIO PROJECT GITHUB COURSE

Project

How do different colonial powers affect government quality of colonised countries?

The main aim of this project is to observe patterns in government quality emerging from countries under the same colonial powers. This project is motivated by the paper The Quality of Government by La Porta et al. (1999), that concluded that legal origins of is a major factor government quality. The research question is explored through different measures of government quality based on former colonies of the British, Spanish and Russian empires. The visualisations used in this project are scatter plots, bar and line charts with sliders and drop-down lists to condense the dataset into a single chart. The value of each observation can be found by hovering over the charts



Data

The data for this project is mainly from the World Bank downloaded using Python in Colab using the API. Datasets from other sources are downloaded directly from TCdata360 and some datasets are cleaned and merged using Stata. The selected sample for the former colonies for the Russian empire were countries under the Soviet Union state, whereas for the British and Spanish are a random selection of countries that were previously colonised by the respective empires. Colonisation by different empires can occurred simultaneously in different areas of a country, therefore the selected sample of countries are defined by the colonisation of the whole land.



Income and Inequality

This visualisation plots the Gini index and the GDP per capita for the two most recent available data. Former colonies of the Russian empire have relatively lower inequality albeit lower income per capita, while for the Spanish inequality is relatively higher. Stata file – cleaning and merging.

Political Freedom

This visualisation plots the press freedom and legal rights scores. The slider allows data for multiple years to be observed. Countries colonised by the Spanish generally have lower legal right scores, but relatively freer press, while colonies under the Russian category have higher legal rights index. Stata file – cleaning and merging.

Perceived Corruption

This visualisation regresses perceived corruption and GDP per capita in 2020 with a polynomial order of 3. Lower perceived corruption correlates to a higher income per capita. Countries under the British and Spanish category vary across both variables, while under the Russian empire, the countries have higher perceived corruption and hence lower GDP per capita. The minor data cleaning and fitted lines were created using Python in Colab and Stata.



Financing Public Goods

This visualisation plots the tax burden, the slider allows selection of observations for a specific year. The highest values for tax burden are developed economies, and no specific patterns emerge for the rest. Typically, higher financing is coupled with higher outcomes of public goods. Stata file – cleaning and merging.

Output of Public Goods

These three visualisations plot the outputs of basic public goods – healthcare, infrastructure and education. Each visualisation includes two separate charts, one of the whole dataset and the other of developing countries, as the higher outcomes of public goods are dominated by developed economies. The sliders enable the selection of a range of years to be observed. As expected, the tax burden data matches the outcomes of public goods – countries with higher tax burden have higher outputs in public goods. Stata file cleaning developing countries data.

Countries under the British category varies largely with relatively higher infant mortality compared to Spanish and Russian. Former Spanish colonies have higher mortality than former Russian colonies. The drop-down list allows the selection of an empire, where any patterns of the countries can be observed.

Former colonies of the Russian empire have relatively higher secure internet servers per 1 million, whereas the other two are scattered countries across the ranks.

Secondary school enrolment for countries under the Russian empire are also relatively higher than the British and Spanish that have high variations across countries. The drop-down list allows the selection of an empire, where any patterns of enrolment can be observed amongst the countries.



Conclusion

Minor patterns emerged for each colonial empire, more so under the Russian empire. The apparent effect on former colonies under the Russian empire is likely since the countries gained independence most recently in 1991. Moreover, the Soviet Union operated as a single country rather than the common definition of colonialism of foreign political control. Surprisingly, countries under the British empire show the least similarities, showing high variation across variables. Therefore, colonial empires impact the government quality of the colonies in varying ways – relatively higher inequality, freer press or higher outcomes of public goods. Nonetheless, every country has different resources, experienced varying degrees of economic crisis and have accumulated centuries of history and culture. These visualisations are only able to describe one factor that affects the variation in government quality over the years.
